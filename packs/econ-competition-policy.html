3.1.1.1 — Economic methodology (amber)

AQA spec requirement
Economics as a social science. Similarities to and differences in methodology from natural and other sciences. The difference between positive and normative statements. How value judgements influence economic decision making and policy.

Social science, not natural science. Economists can't usually run controlled experiments. Behaviour is governed by human choices, which vary over time and context. Theories are tested against data but data is "dirty" — confounding variables, selection effects.

Positive statement: can be tested as true or false against the facts. "Unemployment in the UK was 4.2% in Q1 2025." "A rise in the minimum wage has historically been associated with employment effects of X%."

Normative statement: expresses a value judgement. Cannot be tested against facts alone. "The government should raise the NMW." "Inequality is too high."

Why the distinction matters. Economic policy mixes the two. Deciding to raise NMW is normative (what we ought to do). Estimating the effect of raising NMW on employment is positive (what is). Good economists separate them; bad ones conflate them.

Value judgements are inescapable in policy — they determine what we measure (GDP vs wellbeing?), who counts (current vs future generations?), and what trade-offs we accept (growth vs equality).

3.1.1.4 — Scarcity, choice, resource allocation (amber)

AQA spec requirement
The fundamental economic problem is scarcity and that it results from limited resources and unlimited wants. Scarcity means that choices have to be made about how scarce resources are allocated between different uses. Choices have an opportunity cost.

The fundamental economic problem: resources are finite, wants are infinite, so choices must be made.

Three questions any economy must answer:

  • What to produce? (Cars or schools?)
  • How to produce it? (Labour-intensive or capital-intensive?)
  • For whom to produce? (Rich or poor? Current or future generations?)

Opportunity cost: the value of the next best alternative forgone when making a choice. A government spending £10bn on HS2 foregoes the same £10bn on hospitals, schools, or tax cuts. Opportunity cost is the most important concept in all of economics.

Allocation mechanisms: markets (price signal, incentive, rationing), command (central planning), or mixed economies (most real cases). Each has different efficiency and equity properties.

3.1.8.7 — Competition policy [RED]

AQA spec requirement
The general principles of UK competition policy and some awareness of EU competition policy. The costs and benefits of such policies. Examples of real-world applications of such policies should provide contexts in which students can evaluate the use of economic models to explore economic behaviour. Detailed knowledge of UK and EU competition law is not required.

Why competition policy exists

Monopoly power and anti-competitive practices are sources of market failure:

  • Allocative inefficiency — P > MC, deadweight welfare loss
  • Higher prices for consumers, lower output
  • Productive inefficiency — no competitive pressure to minimise costs (X-inefficiency)
  • Inequality — monopoly rents flow to shareholders (wealthy), higher prices hit everyone
  • Barriers to innovation — entrenched firms may suppress disruption (but Schumpeter counter-argues; see below)

Competition policy tries to correct or prevent these distortions — without going so far as to destroy the legitimate benefits of scale (economies of scale, R&D investment, network effects).

The four pillars of UK competition policy

PillarWhat it targetsExample tools
Merger control Acquisitions that could create/strengthen monopoly CMA review, block / require divestitures / impose conditions
Anti-cartel enforcement Explicit collusion: price-fixing, market-sharing Fines (up to 10% of global turnover), director disqualification, leniency programme
Abuse of dominance Predatory pricing, tying, unfair contracts by dominant firms Behavioural remedies, fines, structural remedies
Market investigations Whole-market inefficiencies even without a single bad actor Industry-wide rule changes (e.g. mobile roaming caps, retail banking remedies)

Who does it — the CMA

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) is the UK's independent competition regulator, created in 2014 (merging the OFT and Competition Commission). It investigates mergers, cartels, abuse of dominance, and consumer protection.

Post-Brexit, CMA handles cases previously referred to the European Commission. It is arguably more powerful now — effectively a global regulator for any merger where UK consumers are affected.

Sector regulators (Ofgem, Ofwat, Ofcom, FCA) have concurrent competition powers in their sectors.

EU competition policy (brief)

The European Commission enforces EU competition law, based on Articles 101 (cartels) and 102 (abuse of dominance) of the TFEU. Similar powers to the CMA, with fines up to 10% of global turnover. Historic big cases: Google Shopping (€2.4bn fine, 2017), Google Android (€4.3bn, 2018), Google AdSense (€1.5bn, 2019). Post-Brexit, EU decisions no longer directly bind UK companies, but UK firms operating in the EU remain subject to EU rules.

Worked case study — Microsoft/Activision 2023

The deal: Microsoft announced in January 2022 a $69bn acquisition of Activision Blizzard — one of the largest tech deals in history. Activision owns Call of Duty, World of Warcraft, Candy Crush.

The CMA's initial decision (April 2023) — BLOCKED.

  • CMA concluded the merger would substantially reduce competition in the nascent cloud gaming market.
  • Microsoft already owned Xbox Cloud Gaming and had a dominant cloud infrastructure position via Azure.
  • Acquiring Activision's content library would give Microsoft market power to disadvantage rival cloud gaming services (Nvidia GeForce Now, Sony PlayStation Cloud).
  • CMA rejected behavioural remedies Microsoft had offered.

The revised decision (October 2023) — APPROVED.

  • Microsoft restructured the deal: divested cloud gaming streaming rights outside the EEA to Ubisoft for 15 years.
  • This removed Microsoft's ability to withhold Activision content from rival cloud services.
  • CMA accepted the restructured deal satisfied competition concerns.
  • Deal closed October 13, 2023.

Why this case matters for the exam.

  • It shows structural remedies (forced divestiture) working — the CMA didn't just demand behavioural promises.
  • It demonstrates the CMA's global reach — both parties US-based, but UK market concerns drove the deal structure.
  • It also shows the difficulty of competition analysis in fast-moving tech markets: regulators must assess competition in markets that are still being created.
  • Use as a contemporary example for any essay on competition policy, monopoly, or market dominance.

Evaluation — does competition policy work?

The case FOR active competition policy

  • Prevents demonstrable welfare loss — blocked price-fixing cartels (e.g. construction bid-rigging, UK supermarket dairy) would have cost consumers billions.
  • Corrects clear market failure where monopoly is entrenched (utilities, telecoms).
  • Deters behaviour even where cases aren't brought — firms self-regulate knowing the CMA exists.
  • Protects small businesses from being squeezed by dominant buyers (e.g. supermarket codes on suppliers).
  • Dynamic benefits — open markets incentivise innovation from new entrants.

The case AGAINST (or for limits on) competition policy

  • Schumpeter / creative destruction. Monopoly profits may fund R&D. Breaking up large firms could reduce dynamic efficiency. Today's monopolist often becomes tomorrow's has-been regardless (see market structures 2 pack).
  • Regulatory capture. Regulators may come to identify with the industries they regulate (e.g. financial services criticism). Ex-regulators often take industry jobs.
  • Information asymmetry. Regulators know less than firms about cost structures, markets, technology. Hard to set the "right" remedy.
  • Chilling effect on beneficial mergers. Some mergers create real synergies. Aggressive blocking may prevent legitimate efficiencies.
  • Global competition. A UK-only regulator may harm UK firms vs international rivals who face less scrutiny.
  • Government failure risk. Competition interventions may themselves create distortions (see 3.1.8.10). Cure might be worse than disease.
Exam gold. The best answers to competition policy questions don't just list CMA tools — they weigh against creative destruction and regulatory capture. You can explicitly cite Schumpeter: "Joseph Schumpeter argued that perennial innovation may erode monopoly more effectively than regulation ever could — as seen with Nokia, Blockbuster, and Kodak." This demonstrates synoptic thinking across 3.1.5.8 and 3.1.8.7.

Two-minute summary

  • Methodology: positive vs normative statements; value judgements shape policy; economics is a social science.
  • Scarcity: limited resources, unlimited wants, choices have opportunity cost.
  • Competition policy = state intervention to maintain competition. Four pillars: merger control, cartel enforcement, abuse of dominance, market investigations.
  • CMA is the UK regulator. EU Commission is the EU equivalent. Post-Brexit CMA is effectively a global player.
  • Key case: Microsoft/Activision 2023 — CMA blocked it, then approved after Microsoft divested cloud rights to Ubisoft. Shows structural remedies work.
  • Evaluation: prevents welfare loss vs Schumpeter's creative destruction case and regulatory capture concerns.
