Section 3.1.8.2 — The meaning of market failure

AQA spec requirement
Market failure occurs whenever a market leads to a misallocation of resources. The difference between complete market failure (resulting in a missing market) and partial market failure (where a market exists but contributes to resource misallocation). How public goods, positive and negative externalities, merit and demerit goods, monopoly and other market imperfections, and inequalities in the distribution of income and wealth can lead to market failure.

Definition cold

Market failure occurs whenever a free market, acting alone, leads to a misallocation of resources — i.e. the market outcome is allocatively inefficient (the quantity produced or consumed is different from the socially optimal level).

Complete market failure

The market doesn't exist at all — a missing market. Usually public goods: no private firm provides national defence, lighthouses, street lighting, because the non-excludability prevents profit.

Partial market failure

The market exists but produces the wrong quantity. Cigarettes are sold, but too many; vaccinations are provided, but too few. This is what externalities typically cause.

The six sources of market failure

  1. Public goods (non-rival, non-excludable) — missing market
  2. Externalities — divergence of private and social cost/benefit
  3. Merit and demerit goods — socially desirable/undesirable goods; often linked to externalities + imperfect info
  4. Monopoly and other market imperfections — P > MC, allocative inefficiency
  5. Factor immobility — resources can't move to where they're needed
  6. Inequality — markets allocate by willingness + ability to pay; excludes those without purchasing power

Section 3.1.8.4 — Externalities

AQA spec requirement
Externalities exist when there is a divergence between private and social costs and benefits. Why negative externalities are likely to result in over-production and that positive externalities are likely to result in under-production. Why the absence of property rights leads to externalities in both production and consumption and hence market failure. Students should be able to illustrate the misallocation of resources resulting from externalities in both production and consumption, using diagrams showing marginal private and social cost and benefit curves.

The four key definitions

TermMeaning
MPC — Marginal Private CostCost to the producer of one more unit
MSC — Marginal Social CostMPC + any external costs to third parties
MPB — Marginal Private BenefitBenefit to the consumer of one more unit
MSB — Marginal Social BenefitMPB + any external benefits to third parties

Key equations:

  • External cost = MSC − MPC (gap between social and private cost curves)
  • External benefit = MSB − MPB (gap between social and private benefit curves)
  • Social optimum: MSB = MSC (society's benefit from last unit equals society's cost)
  • Private market equilibrium: MPB = MPC (consumer willing to pay equals firm's cost to supply)

The entire externalities theory is the gap between these two equilibria.

Four diagrams — you MUST be able to draw all four

Every Paper 1 has at least one externality diagram question. Learn which curve shifts and in which direction for each of the four cases. The deadweight loss (welfare loss) triangle is the examiner's prompt.

Diagram 1 — Negative externality in production

Example: a factory dumps pollution into a river. Third parties (downstream fishers, swimmers, water companies) bear costs the factory doesn't pay.

Market overproduces at Q_mkt. Social optimum is Q*. The red triangle is the deadweight welfare loss — units produced between Q* and Q_mkt cost society more than they benefit society.

Diagram 2 — Negative externality in consumption

Example: smoking. Private smoker gets benefit; passive smokers and the NHS bear costs the smoker doesn't factor in.

MPB overstates social benefit — smokers don't account for third-party harm. Market over-consumes at Q_mkt; optimum is at Q*.

Diagram 3 — Positive externality in consumption

Example: vaccination. The vaccinated person benefits privately; everyone else gains from herd immunity, reduced transmission.

MPB understates social benefit — patients don't factor in herd immunity gains. Market under-consumes at Q_mkt; social optimum Q* is higher.

Diagram 4 — Positive externality in production

Example: a firm's R&D creates knowledge spillovers to other firms (new technologies, trained workers who move on). Third-party firms benefit without paying.

MPC overstates social cost — firm ignores spillovers. Market under-produces at Q_mkt; social optimum Q* is higher. This justifies R&D subsidies and patent protection.

The pattern — memorise this

TypeWhich curve diverges?Social curve is...Market outcome
Neg. externality in productionMSC shifts above MPCHigher (costs more to society)Over-produces
Neg. externality in consumptionMSB shifts below MPBLower (less benefit to society)Over-consumes
Pos. externality in consumptionMSB shifts above MPBHigher (more benefit to society)Under-consumes
Pos. externality in productionMSC shifts below MPCLower (less cost to society)Under-produces
Examiner trick. Students routinely confuse which curve shifts. Rule: externalities in production shift the COST (MSC vs MPC) curve. Externalities in consumption shift the BENEFIT (MSB vs MPB) curve. "Production affects cost, consumption affects benefit." Simple heuristic.

Property rights and the origin of externalities

Why externalities exist. External costs/benefits arise because no one owns the third-party resource being affected. No one owns the atmosphere, so polluting it has no cost to the polluter. No one owns "herd immunity", so vaccinators aren't compensated for providing it.

Coase theorem (1960). If property rights are clearly defined and transaction costs are low, the two parties can negotiate a solution that produces the socially optimal outcome — regardless of who initially holds the rights. Classic example: factory pollutes river near a fishing community. If the fishers have the right to a clean river, the factory pays compensation or reduces pollution. If the factory has the right to pollute, the fishers pay the factory to pollute less. Either way, the efficient outcome is reached.

Limits. Transaction costs high (millions affected), free-riding, asymmetric info, enforcement difficulties. Still, property-rights approach informs carbon markets, fishing quotas, tradeable pollution permits.

Merit and demerit goods (brief — 3.1.8.5)

Goods where consumption generates positive externalities plus imperfect information leads consumers to under-value them. Examples: vaccination, education, exercise. Governments intervene because they judge consumers systematically underestimate private benefits.

Demerit goods: mirror image — negative externalities + consumers underestimate private harm. Smoking, junk food, drugs.

Key distinction: all merit/demerit goods involve externalities, but the defining feature is the value judgement that consumers misjudge their own benefit. Pure externalities don't require that judgement.

Policy responses

PolicyApplies toMechanismProblems
Tax (Pigouvian) Negative externalities Tax = size of external cost. Shifts MPC up to MSC. Hard to measure external cost. Regressive. Evasion.
Subsidy Positive externalities Shifts MPC down or MPB up. Increases output. Opportunity cost of public funds. Risks producer capture.
Regulation / ban Both Mandates behaviour. E.g. MOT emissions, smoking ban in pubs. Blunt. Doesn't use price mechanism. Enforcement costs.
Tradable permits Negative externalities (production) Cap & trade. EU ETS for CO₂. Volatile price. Leakage to uncovered regions.
Property rights / Coase Where parties can negotiate Assign rights, let market price externality Transaction costs high for many-party problems
Information Merit/demerit goods Correct info asymmetry: calorie labels, drink-drive ads Slow. Behavioural biases resist info.

Two-minute summary

  • Market failure = misallocation of resources. Complete (missing market) or partial.
  • Externality = divergence between private and social cost/benefit.
  • Four cases: neg. production → over-produce; neg. consumption → over-consume; pos. consumption → under-consume; pos. production → under-produce.
  • Production shifts COST curves; consumption shifts BENEFIT curves.
  • Welfare loss triangle lies between Q_mkt and Q*, bounded by MSB and MSC.
  • Root cause: missing property rights. Coase theorem shows bargaining can fix this if transaction costs are low.
  • Policy: tax (neg.), subsidy (pos.), regulation, tradeable permits, information provision.

Retrieval test

  1. Define market failure. Distinguish complete vs partial failure with one example each.
  2. Write the four key abbreviations (MPC, MSC, MPB, MSB) and what each means.
  3. State the two equilibrium conditions: market equilibrium and social optimum.
  4. Sketch the negative-externality-in-production diagram. Label both equilibria and the welfare loss triangle.
  5. Sketch the positive-externality-in-consumption diagram. Label welfare loss.
  6. For each of the following, state whether the market over- or under-produces/consumes: factory pollution, smoking, vaccination, R&D spillovers, noise from a pub, education.
  7. State the heuristic: "production shifts the ___ curve; consumption shifts the ___ curve."
  8. Explain Coase theorem in two sentences. Give one real-world example.
  9. Name four policy responses to negative externalities. Give a pro and con of each.
