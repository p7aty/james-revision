Section 3.1.6.1 — The demand for labour

AQA spec requirement
The demand for a factor is derived from the demand for the product. The marginal productivity theory of the demand for labour. The demand curve for labour shows the relationship between the wage rate and number of workers employed. The causes of shifts in the demand curve for labour. The determinants of the elasticity of demand for labour.

Why labour is called "derived demand"

Firms don't want workers for their own sake. They want what workers produce. If nobody wants to buy cars, car manufacturers don't need car workers. This is the foundational idea of the labour market — demand for labour is derived from demand for the product the labour makes.

When the product market booms (iPhones selling well), demand for Apple's workers rises. When the product market collapses (DVDs), demand for workers in that industry falls. Labour demand moves with the goods/services it produces.

Marginal productivity theory

Firms hire workers up to the point where the extra revenue from one more worker equals the extra cost of employing them. In shorthand:

Hire workers while MRP > W. Stop when MRP = W.
  • MRP = Marginal Revenue Product = the extra revenue generated by one more worker
  • MRP = MPP × MR where MPP is marginal physical product (extra output from one more worker) and MR is marginal revenue from selling that output
  • W = wage rate (the marginal cost of one more worker, assuming wages don't change)

Worked example. A café hires baristas. The 5th barista produces 20 extra coffees per shift (MPP = 20). Each coffee sells for £3 (MR = £3). So MRP = 20 × £3 = £60. If the shift wage is £55, the café should hire them. If it's £70, they shouldn't.

This gives us the downward-sloping labour demand curve. As you hire more workers, diminishing marginal returns kick in — each extra worker adds less output than the last. MPP falls, so MRP falls. The firm will only hire more workers at a lower wage rate.

Factors that SHIFT the labour demand curve

Movement along the curve happens when the wage rate changes. A shift of the whole curve happens for other reasons:

FactorEffect on labour demand
Product demand If demand for the product rises (e.g. EVs become popular), MRP rises and labour demand shifts right.
Labour productivity Better training or tech means each worker produces more (MPP rises). Labour demand shifts right.
Price of substitutes (capital) If robots/automation get cheaper, firms substitute capital for labour. Labour demand shifts left.
Price of complements If cheaper capital makes workers more productive (rather than replacing them), labour demand shifts right.
Non-wage employment costs Rises in employer NI contributions, pension contributions, or employment regulation make labour more expensive overall. Labour demand shifts left.
Number of firms in the industry More firms entering = more total demand for that type of labour. Shifts right.

Elasticity of labour demand

How responsive is the quantity of labour demanded to a change in the wage rate? The same determinants as product PED, adapted for labour:

DeterminantMakes demand MORE elastic when...
Ease of substitution Labour is easily replaced by capital (e.g. supermarket checkout staff → self-service machines).
% of total cost that is labour Labour is a large share of total costs (e.g. hospitality). A small wage rise has big impact.
PED of the final product If the product has elastic demand, firms can't pass wage costs on to consumers — so they cut jobs instead.
Time period Longer time = more elastic. In the short run, firms are stuck with current labour. Long run, they can restructure.

Section 3.1.6.2 — The supply of labour

AQA spec requirement
The supply of labour to a particular occupation is influenced by monetary and non-monetary considerations. Non-monetary considerations include job satisfaction and dissatisfaction, and working conditions. The supply curve for labour shows the relationship between the wage rate and number of workers willing to work in an occupation. The causes of shifts in the market supply curve for labour.

Important spec caveat

You are NOT required to understand the determinants of an individual's supply of labour or the backward-bending supply curve. Keep it to the market supply curve only.

The market supply of labour

At higher wage rates, more workers are willing to work in that occupation. Labour flows in from other industries, from unemployment, from further away, from full-time education. The market supply curve for labour slopes upward.

Monetary vs non-monetary considerations

The wage is only part of the story. Workers choose occupations based on:

Monetary

  • The wage rate itself
  • Overtime rates, bonuses, commissions
  • Pension contributions
  • Fringe benefits (company car, health insurance)
  • Job security / income stability

Non-monetary

  • Job satisfaction / sense of purpose
  • Working conditions, safety, hours
  • Location and commute
  • Status and prestige of the job
  • Holiday entitlement, work-life balance
  • Training, promotion prospects

This is why nurses and teachers often accept lower wages than equivalent-skill private sector jobs — the non-monetary rewards are high. And why oil rig workers and deep-sea fishermen earn premiums — the non-monetary costs (danger, isolation) are high.

Factors that SHIFT the labour supply curve

FactorEffect on labour supply
Wages in alternative occupations If another industry raises wages, workers leave. Supply here shifts left.
Non-monetary benefits Improved working conditions, better hours, prestige → supply shifts right.
Barriers to entry Qualifications, licensing, training requirements restrict supply (e.g. doctors). More barriers = supply shifts left / is more inelastic.
Population and migration Growing working-age population or immigration → supply shifts right.
Demographics / social trends More women in workforce, later retirement ages → supply shifts right.
Trade union power / regulation Can restrict supply through closed shops or qualification requirements.
Taxes and benefits Higher income tax can reduce willingness to work; generous benefits can reduce incentive to take low-wage jobs.

Elasticity of labour supply

More elastic when:

  • Low skill requirements — easy for workers to enter the occupation (e.g. bar staff)
  • Short training period — workers can quickly qualify
  • Long time period — more time means more workers can retrain or migrate
  • Plenty of similar alternative occupations — workers can flow in and out easily

Doctors, airline pilots, senior engineers have highly inelastic labour supply. Retail, hospitality, warehousing have elastic supply.

Section 3.1.6.3 — Perfect competition wage determination

AQA spec requirement
The economists' model of wage determination in a perfectly competitive labour market. Role of market forces in determining relative wage rates. Students should appreciate that all real-world markets are imperfectly competitive to a greater or lesser extent.

The assumptions of a perfectly competitive labour market

  • Many firms hiring this type of labour, no individual firm can influence the wage
  • Many workers supplying this type of labour, no individual worker can influence the wage
  • Homogeneous labour — all workers of this type are identical in skill and productivity
  • Perfect information — all workers and firms know wages and conditions everywhere
  • Perfect mobility — workers can freely move between firms without cost
  • No trade unions, no monopsony, no government intervention

The diagram — two panels

This is the diagram you need to draw from memory. It comes in two linked panels:

The industry sets the wage; the individual firm takes it as given. Critical: the firm's supply curve is horizontal.

Reading the diagram — what each piece means

Left panel — the industry

  • The industry's labour demand curve (D) slopes down — aggregate MRP across all firms
  • The industry's labour supply curve (S) slopes up — more workers attracted at higher wages
  • Equilibrium at their intersection sets the market wage W* and market quantity Q*

Right panel — the firm

  • The individual firm is so small relative to the market that it can hire as many workers as it wants at W* — its supply curve is horizontal (perfectly elastic) at W*
  • Because the firm is a wage-taker: S = ACL = MCL (Average Cost of Labour = Marginal Cost of Labour = the going wage)
  • The firm's labour demand curve is its MRP curve
  • The firm hires where MRP = MCL, giving firm-level employment q*
Examiner point. Students often forget that in a perfectly competitive labour market, the firm faces a perfectly elastic (horizontal) labour supply curve. This is THE key distinction from monopsony (where the firm faces an upward-sloping labour supply curve because it IS the market). Get this wrong and you lose the whole analysis.

Why this matters for real-world analysis

Perfect competition in labour markets is a benchmark. Real labour markets rarely satisfy all the assumptions:

  • Workers aren't homogeneous (different skills, productivity)
  • Information is imperfect (workers don't know all vacancies)
  • Mobility is limited (family, housing, retraining costs)
  • Monopsony power exists (one dominant buyer of specific labour)
  • Trade unions exist (collective bargaining)
  • Governments intervene (minimum wages, employment protection)

The PC model gives us the efficient wage and employment level if markets worked perfectly. Deviations from this benchmark produce the misallocations we'll study in 3.1.6.4–3.1.6.7 tomorrow.

Drawing the diagram — three times

Practice sequence

  1. Copy — redraw the diagram above exactly, with the book/notes open. Both panels, all labels.
  2. Look then cover — study it for 30 seconds, then cover it and draw it again from memory. Check what you missed.
  3. From memory — blank page, no reference. Draw both panels, label everything. Include the equations S = ACL = MCL and hire where MRP = MCL.
Mark scheme language. When writing about this in exams, examiners want: "The firm is a wage-taker so faces a perfectly elastic supply of labour at W*. Equilibrium employment occurs where MRP = MCL." Use those words. They trigger the marks.
