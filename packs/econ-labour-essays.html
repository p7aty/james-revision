Labour Market — Essay Plans

Two 25-mark past paper essays, planned in detail

Session purpose

This isn't a teaching pack — it's essay technique. You've done labour market parts 1 and 2 already. Today is about structuring a 25-mark answer under time pressure. Plan-first approach saves exam marks.

  1. 5 min — Read the first essay question. Annotate the command words.
  2. 10 min — Plan the essay (structure below). Do NOT write the full essay — just the plan.
  3. 40 min — Write the full essay under exam conditions.
  4. 15 min — Mark against the mark scheme. Score yourself honestly.
  5. 5 min — Do the same planning exercise for the second question (plan only, no full write-up unless time allows).

Structure for any 25-mark essay

Target: 40 min of writing, 1 side per paragraph approx, 4-5 substantial paragraphs.

  1. Intro (2-3 sentences, 2 min max). Define key terms. State your line of argument. Don't restate the question.
  2. Para 1 — main supporting argument with diagram. Every 25-marker wants at least one diagram. Use it, refer to it explicitly.
  3. Para 2 — second supporting argument or application. Use data from the extract if available.
  4. Para 3 — the "but actually" / evaluation. Counter-argument or nuance. High-scoring answers don't just list points — they weigh them.
  5. Para 4 (optional) — another evaluation strand. Different dimension (time horizon, which consumer groups affected, assumption sensitivity).
  6. Conclusion (3-4 sentences, 2 min). State your overall position. Reference what it depends on. DO NOT just restate everything.
Command words — this determines what examiners want.
  • "Evaluate" — balanced argument with a clear judgement at the end.
  • "Assess" — similar to evaluate; weigh the view given.
  • "Discuss" — explore both sides. Slightly less demanding on the judgement side.
  • "To what extent" — needs a clear answer about how much the proposition holds, not just yes/no.

Essay 1 — Econ 2023 Paper 1 Essay 3

Question: "Evaluate the view that labour markets work best when strong monopsony power is balanced by trade union power." (25 marks)

Tests sections 3.1.6.4 + 3.1.6.5. This is a gift of a question for you — two diagrams you already know, and a clear line of argument available.

Skeleton plan

Intro (3 sentences, ~1½ min)

  • Define monopsony (single dominant buyer) and trade union (collective bargaining body).
  • Outline: the question is whether a union offsetting a monopsonist produces a better outcome than either alone.
  • Preview your position: yes, within a specific range — but not always, and not the only consideration.

Para 1 — Monopsony alone is inefficient [WITH DIAGRAM]

  • Draw the monopsony labour market diagram (MCL above S, firm chooses Q_m where MCL=MRP, pays W_m on S curve).
  • Use the exact mark-scheme language: "employment of q2 workers at a wage of w2, below the perfectly competitive levels and below the MRP".
  • Allocative inefficiency — too few workers hired. Distributional effect — workers exploited.

Para 2 — Union balances this [WITH DIAGRAM]

  • Draw the union-in-monopsony diagram. Show how union wage floor W_u flattens MCL to W_u.
  • Result: both wages AND employment rise (unusual — union is normally a trade-off in PC).
  • Argue this restores the PC outcome within the relevant range. Hence "labour markets work better".

Para 3 — Evaluation: it depends on the union's wage

  • If W_u is set TOO HIGH (above the PC wage), the union overshoots and employment falls below the PC level — same as union-in-PC outcome.
  • The "sweet spot" requires well-informed negotiation. Real unions may push too hard.
  • Cite: UK rail strikes 2023-24 — unions argued for inflation-matching pay; rail employers argued this was unsustainable. Outcome depends on empirical elasticities.

Para 4 — Other evaluation strands

  • Alternative tools exist. NMW achieves similar offset to monopsony power. So unions may not be necessary if NMW is well-set.
  • Worker non-wage conditions. Unions also bargain over hours, safety, holiday — benefits that aren't captured in the simple wage-diagram analysis.
  • Productivity effects. Some unions reduce productivity through restrictive practices (old British Leyland story); some raise it via efficiency wages and training. Not captured in the model.

Conclusion (3-4 sentences)

  • "Balanced power" works when unions negotiate within the sweet spot and when alternative policy (NMW) is absent.
  • Where unions push too hard, the outcome is worse than pure monopsony or pure PC.
  • Policy implication: better to reduce monopsony power directly (break up, competition policy, easier worker mobility) than to rely on countervailing union power.

Essay 2 — Econ 2023 Paper 1 Context question

Question: "Using the extracts and your knowledge of economics, evaluate policies that could be used to reduce in-work poverty in the UK." (25 marks)

Tests sections 3.1.6.6 (NMW), 3.1.7.2 (poverty), 3.1.7.3 (policies). More open-ended — multiple possible policies to evaluate.

Skeleton plan

Intro (3 sentences)

  • Define in-work poverty (working but still below the relative poverty line).
  • Note there are multiple policy tools available — NMW increases, in-work benefits, tax threshold changes, skills policy.
  • Preview: the most effective policy depends on the root cause of the in-work poverty being targeted.

Para 1 — NMW increase [WITH DIAGRAM]

  • Draw the NMW-in-PC diagram showing unemployment as the classical prediction.
  • BUT then do the monopsony diagram showing NMW can raise both wages and employment.
  • Evidence: Low Pay Commission, Card & Krueger. Real UK experience — NMW rises since 1999 haven't caused forecast unemployment.
  • Limitation: helps those in work; does not help the non-employed.

Para 2 — In-work benefits (Universal Credit, tax credits)

  • Top up wages through the benefit system. Direct income transfer.
  • Advantage: targeted at those in need; doesn't risk the theoretical unemployment costs of NMW.
  • Disadvantage: the "taper" creates marginal deduction rates of 50-70%+ — reduces work incentives (classic benefits trap).
  • Also effectively subsidises low-wage employers (Walmart effect).

Para 3 — Skills policy / active labour market

  • Root cause of much in-work poverty is low productivity → low MRP → low wage.
  • Train people into higher-productivity jobs: apprenticeships, FE funding, retraining.
  • Long-run solution but slow. Requires public investment. Skills mismatch problem persistent in UK.

Para 4 — Evaluation: best policy mix depends on cause

  • If in-work poverty is caused by monopsony exploitation: NMW or stronger unions are direct fixes.
  • If caused by low skills and productivity: skills policy, but in-work benefits as a bridge.
  • If caused by high living costs: consider housing policy, childcare subsidy (non-wage) — often the biggest lever for effective income.
  • Trade-offs: short-term vs long-term; incentive effects vs immediate relief; targeted help vs universal.

Conclusion

  • No single policy solves in-work poverty — combination needed.
  • In the short run, NMW + in-work benefits provide immediate lift.
  • In the long run, skills policy and childcare access are the structural fixes.
  • Effectiveness also depends on macro context (unemployment rate, inflation).

Self-mark checklist

After writing, check your essay against these criteria. A 20+/25 (Level 5) answer needs:

  • ✓ Clear argument stated in intro and returned to in conclusion
  • ✓ At least one accurate, labelled diagram used to support analysis
  • ✓ Mark-scheme language — "below competitive employment", "profit-maximising", "allocatively efficient"
  • ✓ Real-world example or extract reference for concrete application
  • ✓ Counter-argument treated seriously, not as a throwaway
  • ✓ Quantified where possible — size of effect, who's affected, time horizon
  • ✓ Clear judgement in conclusion — not "it depends" without saying on what
  • ✓ ~2 sides of writing for 40 minutes (if much less, develop more; if much more, trim)
