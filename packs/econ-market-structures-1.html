Section 3.1.5.2 — Objectives of firms

AQA spec requirement
The models that comprise the traditional theory of the firm are based upon the assumption that firms aim to maximise profits. The profit-maximising rule (MC=MR). The reasons for and the consequences of a divorce of ownership from control. Firms have a variety of other possible objectives. The satisficing principle.

The profit-maximising rule: MC = MR

Traditional theory assumes firms want to maximise profit. The profit-maximising output is where marginal cost equals marginal revenue. Why?

  • If MR > MC, producing one more unit adds more to revenue than to cost — profit rises. Keep producing.
  • If MC > MR, the last unit cost more than it brought in — profit falls. Produce less.
  • When MC = MR, any change in output makes you worse off. That's the maximum.

Add one subtlety: MC must be cutting MR from below (MC rising through MR). If MC is cutting from above, you're at a minimum of profit, not a maximum.

Divorce of ownership from control

In small firms, the owner runs the business — motives are aligned with profit. In large firms, shareholders (owners) and managers (controllers) are different people. This is the principal-agent problem.

Shareholders want...Managers may actually pursue...
Maximum long-run profit / dividendsRevenue max (pay often tied to sales)
Efficient cost controlGrowth / empire building (more staff = higher status)
High return on capitalManagerial perks (offices, travel)
Long-term strategyShort-term share price (to trigger bonuses)
Risk-taking where profitableRisk aversion (career preservation)

Consequences: firms may not actually maximise profit. Agency costs arise — shareholders spend on monitoring (audits, NEDs, performance-linked pay) to keep managers aligned. Governance reforms (Cadbury 1992, combined code, say-on-pay votes) all target this.

Other possible objectives

  • Sales / revenue maximisation — output where MR = 0 (total revenue at its peak). Baumol's model — managers often remunerated on revenue.
  • Sales-volume maximisation — sell as many units as possible subject to a minimum profit constraint. Market-share driver.
  • Growth maximisation — managerial empire-building (Marris model).
  • Survival — especially in downturns or for startups; staying solvent beats short-term profit.
  • Quality or reputation — premium brands (Rolls-Royce, Hermès) sometimes refuse profit-maximising price cuts.
  • Social / ethical objectives — B-Corps, Patagonia's "1% for the planet", worker cooperatives.
  • Satisficing — Herbert Simon. With bounded rationality, managers can't optimise across all options, so they aim for an "acceptable" outcome satisfying all stakeholders (shareholders, workers, customers, regulators) — not the maximum on any single dimension.
Examiner point. When asked "does the profit-maximising assumption hold in the real world?" the good answer lists alternative objectives (sales max, revenue max, satisficing) AND the principal-agent problem that explains why they exist. Then you can evaluate: profit max may still apply in the long run because persistent under-performance gets management replaced.

Section 3.1.5.3 — Perfect competition

AQA spec requirement
The formal diagrammatic analysis of the perfectly competitive model in the short and long run. The implications of the following for the behaviour of firms and the industry: large numbers of producers, identical products, freedom of entry and exit, and perfect knowledge. Firms operating in perfectly competitive markets are price takers. The proposition that, given certain assumptions, relating for example to a lack of externalities, perfect competition will result in an efficient allocation of resources.

The five assumptions

  1. Many buyers and sellers — no single participant influences price
  2. Homogeneous product — every firm sells an identical good
  3. Free entry and exit — no barriers to setting up or leaving
  4. Perfect information — everyone knows all prices, costs, technology
  5. No externalities, no government intervention — a clean benchmark

Real-world examples that come close: agricultural commodities (wheat, coffee beans), FX markets, some digital advertising auctions. No market is truly perfectly competitive — PC is a benchmark against which to measure real markets.

The firm is a price-taker

Because the firm is so small relative to the market, it can sell any quantity at the prevailing market price. Sell above and you get zero demand (identical product elsewhere). Sell below and you lose money you didn't need to.

Consequence: the firm's demand curve (AR) is horizontal at the market price. And because every unit sells at the same price, MR = AR = P.

The short-run diagram — three possible positions

In the short run, firms can earn abnormal profit, break even, or make a loss. The market determines P; the firm produces where MC = MR; profit depends on where ATC sits relative to P.

Short-run: firms produce where MC = MR (= P). Profit is the difference between P and ATC, multiplied by output. Three cases depending on where ATC sits.

The shutdown condition

When the firm is making a loss, should it continue producing or shut down? Look at the AVC curve (average variable cost):

  • If P ≥ AVC: keep producing. Revenue covers variable costs AND contributes something to fixed costs. Closing would mean losing all of the fixed costs with nothing offsetting them.
  • If P < AVC: shut down. Every unit produced adds more variable cost than revenue. Better to stop and only lose the fixed costs.

The shutdown point is where P = minimum AVC. Below this, close. Above it, keep going even at a loss.

The long-run diagram — why abnormal profit disappears

In the long run, firms can enter or exit the industry. This is the mechanism that drives PC towards its characteristic outcome.

Long-run: abnormal profits attract new firms, shifting industry supply right, lowering P. Process continues until P just touches min(ATC). Firm makes normal profit only.

The adjustment story

  1. Start: firms in the industry are making supernormal profit (case a above).
  2. New firms enter — attracted by abnormal profit, free entry allows this.
  3. Industry supply shifts right (more firms producing).
  4. Market price falls along fixed demand.
  5. Each firm's horizontal demand drops with the market price.
  6. Profit per firm falls.
  7. Process continues until P = min(ATC) — no abnormal profit left, so no new entrants. Equilibrium.

The mirror process runs the other way starting from losses: firms exit, supply shifts left, price rises, until surviving firms break even.

Efficiency outcomes of PC (the key result)

Given the assumptions hold, long-run PC achieves:

  • Productive efficiency — production occurs at min(ATC), so cost per unit is as low as possible. Firms that don't achieve this are undercut and exit.
  • Allocative efficiency — P = MC, so the price consumers pay for the last unit equals the marginal cost of producing it. No deadweight loss.
  • No abnormal profit — consumer surplus is maximised; the firm captures no rent.
  • But: limited dynamic efficiency — firms have no abnormal profit to invest in R&D. This is the weakness of PC as a benchmark.
Mark scheme language. "In long-run equilibrium, perfect competition is both productively efficient (firms produce at min ATC) and allocatively efficient (P = MC). However, the absence of abnormal profit limits investment in research and development, suggesting perfect competition may lack dynamic efficiency." That paragraph — almost verbatim — hits top-band marks.

Critical evaluation

  • The assumptions rarely hold — real markets have product differentiation, barriers to entry, imperfect info. PC is a benchmark, not a description.
  • No innovation incentive — pure PC discourages R&D because any innovation is immediately copied (free entry + perfect info). This is why Schumpeter argued some monopoly power may be good for dynamic efficiency.
  • Consumer choice is low — identical products everywhere. Non-price competition (brand, quality, service) is forbidden by the homogeneous-product assumption.
  • Firm size is sub-optimal — if there are economies of scale, PC forces firms to stay too small to exploit them.
  • Real-world proximity: wheat, FX, global commodities. Firms in these markets do behave close to the PC predictions — thin margins, price-taking, constant efficiency pressure.

Two-minute summary

  • Profit-max rule: MC = MR (with MC cutting MR from below).
  • Principal-agent problem: managers may not profit-max — alternative objectives like sales max, growth, satisficing.
  • Perfect competition: 5 assumptions. Firms are price-takers. AR = MR = P is horizontal.
  • Short run: three cases — abnormal profit, break-even, loss. Shut down if P < AVC.
  • Long run: free entry/exit drives P down to min(ATC). Normal profit only.
  • Efficiency: PC gives productive + allocative efficiency BUT weak dynamic efficiency (no R&D funds).
