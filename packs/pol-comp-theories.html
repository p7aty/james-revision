Comparative Theories [RED]

Rational · Cultural · Structural approaches

90-minute session
  1. 10 min — Learn the three theories cold with associated thinkers.
  2. 30 min — Work through worked examples applying each to UK/US differences.
  3. 30 min — Build a grid of examples: for each theory, 3 UK/US comparisons across institutions.
  4. 20 min — Attempt one 9-marker from a past paper.

Why this is red: COMP-1 is abstract theory, not memorable facts. Easy to lose marks by mechanical application. Goal today = being able to reach for the right theory reflexively and apply it with a concrete UK vs US example.

Where this fits in the exam

Paper 2 (US & Comparative) Section A opens with 9-mark questions of the form:

"Explain and analyse three ways in which [rational/cultural/structural] theory could be used to study [executive-legislative relations / party systems / voting behaviour / etc.] in the UK and US."

AQA's own teachers' guide states the three definitions you need. These are the words to use:

  • Structural theory (Parsons, Almond): comparative government refers to the differing structure of institutions — constitutions, governmental systems — and how these shape political behaviour.
  • Rational theory (Blondel): compares the differing political choices that individuals make as they pursue their self-interest — voters, politicians, pressure groups as rational actors.
  • Cultural theory: compares how the differing histories and shared ideas/beliefs of the two countries have shaped the "tone" of political debate and activity.
Mark-scheme clarification. For 9-markers, you do NOT need to draw an explicit UK–US comparison. You need to explain HOW each theory could be used to study a given topic — giving examples from both countries separately is enough. The 25-mark questions require proper comparative analysis.

Theory 1 — Structural

Core idea: Institutional differences shape political outcomes. Parsons and Almond argued politics is best understood by studying the formal and informal structures (constitution, legislature structure, separation of powers, federal vs unitary arrangements).

Exam phrase to use: "Structural theory, associated with Parsons and Almond, analyses comparative politics through the lens of institutions, processes, and constitutional arrangements."

Applied to UK vs US — three worked examples

Ex 1 — Executive-legislative relations.

  • UK: fusion of powers — the PM sits in the legislature, commands the Commons majority, so executive decisions usually pass.
  • US: separation of powers — president and Congress elected separately; divided government routinely produces deadlock (shutdowns, failed Supreme Court appointments).
  • Structural point: the different constitutional structure — not the personalities — explains divergent effectiveness of executive action.

Ex 2 — Judicial power.

  • UK: Supreme Court is relatively new (2009). No power to strike down primary legislation (parliamentary sovereignty). Judicial review of executive actions but not Acts.
  • US: SCOTUS has had power of judicial review since Marbury v Madison (1803). Can strike down federal and state laws. Entrenched constitutional role.
  • Structural point: the codified vs uncodified constitution creates different judicial structures.

Ex 3 — Pressure group influence.

  • UK: centralised executive (Cabinet + PM) is the main decision point. Pressure groups target Whitehall, No 10, specific ministers.
  • US: separation of powers creates multiple access points — Congress, White House, courts, 50 state legislatures. Pressure groups can lobby at every stage (and often do).
  • Structural point: more veto players in the US structure means more points of access — and more chances to block legislation.

Theory 2 — Rational

Core idea: Political actors — voters, politicians, interest groups — are assumed to act in their self-interest, making calculated choices to maximise utility. Blondel is the key thinker.

Exam phrase to use: "Rational theory, associated with Blondel, explains political outcomes as the aggregate of individual actors making calculated choices to maximise their self-interest."

Applied to UK vs US — three worked examples

Ex 1 — Pressure group influence.

  • US: pressure groups are more influential than in the UK. Rational explanation — US election finance rules allow direct donations to candidates, so politicians respond more to donor interests (rational choice: my re-election depends on the money).
  • UK: strict party funding limits; parties (not individual candidates) control election finance. Pressure groups influence parties collectively rather than individual MPs.
  • Rational point: different incentive structures → different levels of individual politician responsiveness to pressure groups.

Ex 2 — Voting behaviour.

  • UK: FPTP creates rational incentive for tactical voting (vote for your second-choice party to block your least favourite). St Albans 2019 — voters switched from Labour to Lib Dem to block Conservative.
  • US: similar rational incentive in primaries (Trump 2016 strategic voting) and in two-party general elections. Third-party votes seen as wasted.
  • Rational point: voters maximise expected utility given the electoral system — they're not expressing pure preference but strategic preference.

Ex 3 — Politician decision-making under party disunity.

  • UK: Sunak compromised with his party's right on immigration to preserve his leadership. Rational: his own political survival depended on keeping the party wing on-side.
  • US: Biden compromised on climate in the 2022 Inflation Reduction Act to get Manchin's vote. Rational: passing something beats passing nothing.
  • Rational point: leaders make policy trade-offs based on calculated self-interest in political survival, not pure ideology.

Theory 3 — Cultural

Core idea: Political behaviour is shaped by shared histories, values, and beliefs of groups within a country. Culture provides the context within which political choices are made.

Exam phrase to use: "Cultural theory analyses politics through the differing histories, values, and shared beliefs of different countries, which shape the tone and content of political debate."

Applied to UK vs US — three worked examples

Ex 1 — Why socialism is weaker in the US.

  • UK: strong labour movement tradition (industrial revolution, trade unions, Labour Party from 1900). Socialism part of mainstream political culture.
  • US: founded on individualism, frontier self-reliance, limited government. Socialism carries cold-war baggage (Red Scare, McCarthyism). Even progressive candidates (Bernie Sanders) face cultural resistance.
  • Cultural point: historical origin and shared values determine what kinds of politics are even imaginable.

Ex 2 — Attitude to hereditary institutions.

  • UK: tolerates and romanticises hereditary monarchy, House of Lords with remaining hereditary peers. Cultural continuity with pre-democratic institutions.
  • US: founded on egalitarian revolt against hereditary privilege. Even the suggestion of royal/noble titles is unthinkable; Article I of the Constitution bans them.
  • Cultural point: divergent histories — imported vs revolutionary — produce fundamentally different tolerance of inherited political status.

Ex 3 — Role of religion in politics.

  • US: religion (especially evangelical Christianity) deeply entwined with politics. Abortion, school prayer, "God bless America" are mainstream political issues. Most presidents explicitly religious.
  • UK: more secular. Thatcher, Blair, Cameron were personally religious but religion was not a campaigning issue. "We don't do God" — Alastair Campbell.
  • Cultural point: different religious histories (US as refuge from religious persecution; UK's established but weakened Church of England) produce different political roles for religion.

How to structure a 9-mark answer

"Explain and analyse three ways rational theory could be used to study pressure groups in the UK and US."

Standard structure — 9 marks = 9 min writing, so brief:

  1. Sentence 1 (definition of theory): "Rational theory, associated with Blondel, analyses comparative politics through the view that informed political decisions are driven by actors' self-interest."
  2. Point 1 (2-3 sentences): [describe one way + give US and UK examples] — e.g. campaign finance incentives.
  3. Point 2 (2-3 sentences): [second way] — e.g. pressure group membership as individual calculation of influence per £.
  4. Point 3 (2-3 sentences): [third way] — e.g. politicians rationally responding to pressure group pressure if it affects their re-election.

~1 page of writing total. Don't over-develop — this isn't an essay. Clarity and correct application of the theory beats depth.

Two-minute summary

  • Structural (Parsons, Almond) — institutions, constitutional structures, processes.
  • Rational (Blondel) — individual actors pursuing self-interest through calculated choices.
  • Cultural — shared history, values, and beliefs of groups within a country.
  • Paper 2 Section A = 9-mark questions applying one theory to a specific topic. No explicit comparison needed.
  • Paper 2 Section B = 25-mark essays requiring full comparative analysis.
  • Always: start with a one-sentence definition of the theory, then three applications with UK and US examples.
